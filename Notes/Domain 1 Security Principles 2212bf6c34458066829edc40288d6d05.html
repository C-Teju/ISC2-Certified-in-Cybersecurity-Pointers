Risk Identification

Risk identification in cybersecurity is an ongoing process, not a one-time event. It involves:

• Continuously identifying, characterizing, and estimating risks to the organization
• Analyzing the organization's unique situation and plans
• Looking beyond obvious dangers to find potential problems

Key takeaways:
• Clear risk communication is essential
• Risk identification is everyone's responsibility
• Security professionals typically focus on system-level risk assessment
• In smaller organizations, security professionals may help develop missing risk management plans

Risk Priorities

Identified risks needs to be prioritized and analyzed core through qualitative and/or quantitative risk analysis. This is necessary to determine the root cause and narrow down apparent risks and core risks. Security professionals work with their teams to conduct both qualitative and quantitative analysis.

Understanding an organization’s mission and supporting functions helps place risks in context and prioritize them effectively. Management typically guides how risk assessment findings should inform actions. A useful tool for prioritizing risks is the risk matrix, which evaluates risks based on likelihood and impact, providing a common framework for decision-making. For instance, high likelihood and high impact risks are given high priority, while low likelihood and low impact risks are low priority. Final priorities may also consider business goals, mitigation costs, and potential losses.

Governance Elements

When leaders and management implement the systems and structures that organizations use to achieve their goals, they follow laws and regulations created by governments to enact public policy. These laws and regulations guide the development of standards, which form policies, which in turn lead to specific procedures.

Procedures are the detailed steps to complete a task that support departmental or organizational policies.

● Policies are established by organizational governance, such as executive management, to provide guidance across all activities and ensure the organization adheres to industry standards and regulations.

● Standards are frameworks used by governance teams to develop and implement policies and procedures that support regulatory compliance.

● Regulations are typically issued as laws by government bodies (distinct from governance) and often carry financial penalties for non-compliance.

Regulations

Regulations and associated fines and penalties can be imposed by governments at the national, regional, or local level. Because regulations and laws can be imposed and enforced differently in different parts of the world, here are a few examples to connect the concepts to actual regulations

  • The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 is an example of a law that governs the use of protected health information (PHI) in the United States.
  • The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) was enacted by the European Union (EU) to control use of Personally Identifiable Information (PII) of its citizens and those in the EU

Finally, it is common to be subject to regulation on several levels. Multinational organizations are subject to regulations in more than one nation in addition to multiple regions and municipalities. Organizations need to consider the regulations that apply to their business at all levels—national, regional, and local—and ensure they are compliant with the most restrictive regulation.

Standards

Organizations use various standards in their information security programs for compliance and guidance. These standards ensure policies align with regulations and best practices.

  • ISO (International Organization for Standardization) publishes global standards on information systems, security, and encryption, with expert input. ISO standards must be purchased online.
  • NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology), a U.S. government agency, provides free, widely respected IT and cybersecurity standards, often mandatory for federal agencies.
  • IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force) creates communication protocol standards that enable global computer connectivity, regardless of language differences.
  • IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) sets standards in telecommunications, computer engineering, and related fields.

These standards ensure secure, compatible, and effective IT systems worldwide.

Policies

Policies are broad, high-level statements informed by laws and regulations that guide an organization's direction, priorities, and decision-making. Governance policies ensure compliance and shape other policies across departments like HR, finance, and security.

Senior executives use these policies to steer the organization toward its goals. When compliance is required by law or contracts, specific high-level policies are developed and assessed for effectiveness.

To implement policies, they must be translated into detailed procedures that provide step-by-step instructions for action.

Procedures

Procedures are detailed, repeatable steps for completing specific tasks. They include necessary data, decision criteria, and success metrics. Procedures may cover routine or one-time actions and must be well-documented and taught to staff for effective implementation.

Non-repudiation

Non-repudiation is a legal term defined as the protection against an individual falsely denying having performed a particular action.

Non-repudiation ensures individuals cannot deny actions they performed, such as sending messages or making transactions. It is crucial in digital environments to prevent impersonation and build trust by holding users accountable for their actions.
