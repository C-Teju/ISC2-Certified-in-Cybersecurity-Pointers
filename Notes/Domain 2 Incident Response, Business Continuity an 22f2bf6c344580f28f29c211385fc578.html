Domain 2: Incident Response, Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery Concepts

Incident Terminology

  • Breach: The loss of control, compromise, unauthorized disclosure, unauthorized acquisition, or any similar occurrence where: a person other than an authorized user accesses or potentially accesses personally identifiable information; or an authorized user accesses personally identifiable information for other than an authorized purpose. NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5
  • Event: Any observable occurrence in a network or system. NIST SP 800-61 Rev 2
  • Exploit: A particular attack. It is named this way because these attacks exploit system vulnerabilities.
  • Incident: An event that actually or potentially jeopardizes the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an information system or the information the system processes, stores, or transmits.
  • Intrusion: A security event, or combination of events, that constitutes a deliberate security incident in which an intruder gains, or attempts to gain, access to a system or system resource without authorization. IETF RFC 4949 Ver 2
  • Threat: Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organizational operations (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), organizational assets, individuals, other organizations, or the nation through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification of information, and/or denial of service. NIST SP 800-30 Rev 1
  • Vulnerability: Weakness in an information system, system security procedures, internal controls, or implementation that could be exploited by a threat source. NIST SP 800-30 Rev 1
  • Zero Day: A previously unknown system vulnerability with the potential of exploitation without risk of detection or prevention because it does not, in general, fit recognized patterns, signatures, or methods.

The Goal of Incident Response

The priority of any incident response is to protect life, health, and safety. When any decision related to priorities is to be made, always choose safety first.

The primary goal of incident management is to be prepared. Preparation requires having a policy and a response plan that will lead the organization through the crisis. Some organizations use the term “crisis management” to describe this process.

An event is any measurable occurrence, usually harmless. If it threatens business operations, it's called an incident. Organizations need an incident response plan to minimize impact and restore operations quickly. This planning is part of business continuity management (BCM).

What term is sometimes used interchangeably with "incident management"?
Crisis Management

Business Continuity in the Workplace

Business continuity plans are usually digital, but relying solely on them can be risky during emergencies like power outages. To prepare, some organizations maintain a hard copy "red book" with critical procedures, stored off-site. It must be updated alongside the digital version to ensure consistency.

What is the purpose of a red book in the context of business continuity?
To serve as a hard copy backup accessible outside the facility

Business Continuity Plan (BCP)

Business Continuity Planning (BCP) involves proactively preparing procedures to restore operations following a disaster or major disruption. Members from across the organization should participate in creating the BCP to ensure all systems, processes, and operations are accounted for in the plan. It is primarily a business function, but must align with technology to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA) of information.

Core Components:
BCP Team List

  • Names of BCP team including multiple contact methods
  • Backup personnel included

Management Guidance

  • Clear authority designations for key managers

Critical Contacts in supply chain

  • Vendors, customers, emergency services, and third-party partners

Plan Activation Criteria

  • Clear guidelines on when and how to initiate the BCP

Immediate Response Procedures

  • Safety and security protocols
  • Fire suppression procedures
  • Emergency agency notifications
  • Communication Framework

Notification systems

  • Call trees to alert personnel and stakeholders

Business Continuity in Action

A fire destroyed the billing department overnight. No personnel were harmed. A Business Impact Analysis (BIA) conducted four months prior had identified billing as important but not immediately critical.

Thanks to pre-planning:

An alternate worksite was arranged in advance and will be operational within a week.

Customer service is handling billing inquiries in the meantime.

Billing staff will work from the temporary site until a permanent space is ready.

The company’s cash reserves allow for a week-long delay in billing without financial stress. Operations and customer service remained unaffected, demonstrating a successful execution of the Business Continuity Plan (BCP).

What is a key outcome of a Business Impact Analysis (BIA)?

Identification of functions and dependencies

Components of the Incident Response Plan

The incident response policy should reference a detailed, role-based incident response plan. This plan includes procedures, standards, and tools to guide incident handling and is regularly updated. It should align with the organization’s vision, mission, and strategy, and define the technical steps, techniques, and checklists for effective response.

Preparation

  • Develop a policy approved by management.
  • Identify critical data and systems and any single points of failure.
  • Train staff on incident response.
  • Implement an incident response team.
  • Practice incident identification (first response).
  • Identify roles and responsibilities.
  • Plan the coordination of communication between stakeholders.
  • Consider the possibility that a primary method of communication may not be available.

Detection and Analysis

  • Monitor all possible attack vectors.
  • Analyze the incident using known data and threat intelligence.
  • Prioritize incident response.
  • Standardize incident documentation.

Containment

  • Gather evidence.
  • Choose an appropriate containment strategy.
  • Identify the attacker.
  • Isolate the attack.

Post-Incident Activity

  • Identify evidence that may need to be retained.
  • Document lessons learned.

Conduct a retrospective of:

  • Preparation
  • Detection and Analysis
  • Containment, Eradication, and Recovery
  • Post-incident Activity
What is the first phase in the components of an incident response plan?
Preparation

The Goal of Disaster Recovery

Disaster recovery planning begins where business continuity (BC) ends.
While BC focuses on keeping critical business functions running during a disruption, disaster recovery (DR) focuses on restoring IT and communication systems after the disruption.

The disaster recovery plan (DRP) guides emergency teams in restoring systems to their last known reliable state.
Although business functions can sometimes be restored without IT, IT recovery is usually essential for full business recovery.

What is the primary distinction between business continuity planning (BCP) and disaster recovery planning (DRP)?
DRP is about restoring IT, while BCP focuses on business operations
Which of the following is very likely to be used in a disaster recovery (DR) effort?
Data backups

Components of a Disaster Recovery Plan

Depending on the organization's size and the number of people involved in the disaster recovery planning (DRP), multiple plan documents may be created for different audiences.

These may include:

  • An executive summary with a high-level overview of the plan
  • Department-specific recovery plans
  • Technical guides for IT personnel to manage backup systems and restore services
  • Full copies of the plan for critical disaster recovery team members

Each group receives documents tailored to their role:

  • disaster recovery planning (DRP) team members get checklists to follow during a disaster
  • IT staff use technical guides to activate alternate sites
  • Managers and PR teams use simplified high-level documents to communicate clearly without interrupting team members who are busy working on the recovery.

What is the purpose of the Executive Summary in a Disaster Recovery Plan?
To offer a high-level overview of the plan

The Goal of Business Continuity

Business continuity ensures that an organization’s critical functions can continue, even at reduced capacity, during disruptions such as cyberattacks, system failures, or natural disasters. While minor incidents may require only basic response, major disruptions need a full business continuity approach.

Business continuity focuses on essential services, enabling them to operate until normal operations are restored. It involves planning, preparation, response, and recovery—but not full restoration. Successful continuity planning requires strong support from executive management and a commitment of resources.
