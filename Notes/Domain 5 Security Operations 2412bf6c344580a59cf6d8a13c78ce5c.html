Data Handling Practices

Data has value and must be handled appropriately. We will explore the basics of classifying and labeling data to ensure it is treated and controlled in a manner consistent with the sensitivity of the data. In addition, we will complete the data life cycle by documenting retention requirements and ensuring data that is no longer in use is destroyed.

Classification

  • Purpose: Protect valuable information from being stolen, leaked, or misused.
  • Definition: Classification is assessing the potential impact if information is compromised in confidentiality, integrity, or availability, then labeling and handling it accordingly.
  • Process:
    1. Assess potential loss or impact to the organization.
    1. Apply labels to indicate sensitivity and handling requirements.
  • Sources of classification rules: Laws, regulations, contracts, or business standards.
  • Levels of impact: Can range from minor disruption to grave consequences (e.g., loss of life, business collapse).
  • Benefit: Allows consistent and efficient security controls for similarly classified data.

Labeling

  • Purpose: Assign sensitivity levels to data so protection matches the potential harm from compromise.
  • Principle: Higher sensitivity = higher security requirements.
  • Best practice:
    • Use 2‚Äì4 sensitivity levels (too many can cause confusion).
    • Labels must be clear and consistent.
  • Common Sensitivity Levels:
    1. Highly Restricted ‚Äì Compromise could risk organization‚Äôs survival, cause major harm, or lead to lawsuits.
    1. Moderately Restricted ‚Äì Compromise could cause loss of competitive advantage, revenue, or disrupt operations.
    1. Low Sensitivity ‚Äì Compromise causes only minor disruptions (e.g., internal use only).
    1. Unrestricted/Public ‚Äì Already public; no harm from disclosure.

Retention

  • Purpose: Keep data only as long as it‚Äôs beneficial or legally required.
  • Requirements:
    • Some industries/laws set mandatory retention periods.
    • If no law applies, the organization must define its own retention policy.
    • Applies to both hard copy and electronic data.
  • Responsibilities of Security Professionals:
    • Ensure timely destruction once retention limit is reached.
    • Maintain accurate inventory (asset location, retention period, destruction method).
    • Periodically review records to remove unnecessary data.
  • Retention Policies Should:
    1. Ensure personnel understand retention requirements for all data types.
    1. Document retention requirements for each information type.
    1. Guarantee data is kept only for the required duration, then securely destroyed.
  • Common Mistake in Retention
    1. Applying the longest retention period to all data is a bad practice.
    1. Risks:
      • Wastes storage.
      • Increases exposure risk.
      • Creates extra ‚Äúnoise‚Äù in searches.
      • May violate laws, regulations, or contracts ‚Üí could lead to fines.
    1. Best Practice:
      • Destroy records no longer required, following enterprise policy and legal requirements.

Destruction

  • Remanence: Residual data left after deletion; can be a security risk.
  • Mitigation Methods:
    1. Clearing (Overwriting/Zeroizing): Write random patterns across storage. Risk: missed blocks may still contain recoverable data.
    1. Purging: Removes residual physical traces after clearing (e.g., degaussing magnetic media). More secure than clearing.
    1. Physical Destruction: Shredding, acid etching, burning, or breaking media; ensures data cannot be recovered.
  • Operational Practices:
    • Clearing may be enough for routine use.
    • Purging or destruction required for sensitive data before disposal or hardware replacement.

Logging and Monitoring Security Events

Purpose & Importance

  • Logging: Captures signals from system events for accountability and analysis.
  • Events: Any action in the system causing measurable changes.
  • Value: Helps detect compromises, policy violations, fraud, and operational issues.

What to Log

  • User IDs
  • System activities
  • Date/time of key events (e.g., logon/logoff)
  • Device & location details
  • Successful and failed access attempts
  • System configuration changes
  • Activation/deactivation of protection mechanisms

Benefits

  • Supports audits and forensic investigations.
  • Correlates data from different systems for better incident analysis.
  • Helps identify system inefficiencies and vulnerabilities.

Best Practices

  • Regular log reviews.
  • Build a log management infrastructure to:
    • Preserve log integrity and confidentiality.
    • Prevent unauthorized modifications or deletions.
  • Monitor storage capacity to avoid log loss.
  • Follow legal/regulatory log retention policies.
  • Preserve original logs to prevent attackers from erasing evidence.

Data Security Event Example

  • Raw logs can reveal attempts to break into secure files or hijack servers.
  • Security engineers interpret log codes to identify:
    • Who attempted the access
    • Whether a secure or suspicious port was used
  • Some tools provide more user-friendly monitoring, but raw log familiarity is still valuable.
  • Security must be planned from the start ‚Äî not added as a quick fix.
  • System patching and updates help, but without a secure foundation, protection will be weak.

Encryption Overview

  • Purpose:
    • Protects personal/business transactions.
    • Verifies authenticity of software updates and contracts.
    • Keeps information unintelligible without the correct decryption key.
  • Process:
    • Plaintext (original data) ‚Üí encrypted into ciphertext (unreadable form).
    • Only authorized parties can decrypt and read.
  • Core Security Services:
    1. Confidentiality ‚Äì Ensures only authorized recipients can read the message.
    1. Integrity ‚Äì Hashes & digital signatures verify data hasn‚Äôt been altered.
  • Encryption System Components:
    • Hardware, software, algorithms, control parameters, operational methods.
  • Plaintext Examples:
    • Images, audio, video (raw or compressed).
    • Human-readable text or numeric data (with/without formatting metadata).
    • Database files or fields.
    • Any digital data, even if not human-readable.

Encryption Deep Dive

  • Historical Use:
    • Dates back to Magura cave drawings and Egyptian pyramids.
    • Used to keep secrets (e.g., hunting grounds, resources) from rival groups.
  • Basic Process:
    1. Start with clear text (readable info).
    1. Apply an algorithm to convert it into ciphertext (unreadable).
    1. Unlock using an encryption key.
  • Modern Example:
    • Encrypting a PDF from your accountant before sending tax documents.
    • Only sender and receiver with the preset key can access it.
  • Key Management:
    • Safeguard keys to prevent unauthorized access.
    • In business, keys often stored & managed by a third party or external server.
    • Protected with hashing for added security.
  • Asymmetric Encryption:
    • Sender & receiver each have unique keys (often digital certificates).
    • Confirms sender‚Äôs identity and ensures secure delivery.
Why is asymmetric encryption considered more secure?
It involves a unique code for the sender and receiver

How Passwords Work

  • Passwords are stored as hash values (digests), not in plain text.
  • A secure password with letters, numbers, and symbols generates a unique hash.
  • Systems check passwords by comparing hash values, not the actual password.
  • Users are often required to create strong passwords (minimum length, complexity).
  • Attackers can steal hashed password files and use offline attacks (e.g., brute force) to guess them.
  • If they know the hash algorithm, they can try random combinations until the hash matches.

Hashing Deep Dive

  • Definition: Hashing converts data into a fixed-length alphanumeric string (digest) using a hash function. In hashing, the digest (output) length is fixed for a given hash algorithm, no matter how big or small the input is.
  • Key Property: Any small change in input creates a completely different digest.
  • Purpose: Confirms data integrity‚Äîensures data hasn‚Äôt been altered.
  • Example: If a payment amount changes from 5000 to 50,000 then the digest changes, alerting to tampering.
  • Use in Software: Compare a provided digest (checksum) with the original to detect unauthorized changes.
  • Real Case: At the University of Florida, Windows 2000 or Millennium, was provided to 50,000 students via CD-ROMs - compromised software CDs were detected due to mismatched digests.
How does hashing respond to minor changes in the input, such as misspellings or changes in letter case?
It generates a different hash digest for each input

  • Extra Defense: Helps detect both accidental and malicious alterations before data or software is deployed.

Configuration Management Overview

  • Purpose: Ensures only authorized and validated changes are made to a system.
  • Key Components:
    1. Identification: Define the system‚Äôs baseline (components, interfaces, documentation).
    1. Baseline: Minimum security standard used as a reference for updates.
    1. Change Control: Process to request, review, approve, and apply updates or patches.
    1. Verification & Audit: Test and validate changes; ensure system matches the baseline + approved changes.

1. Inventory

  • First step in asset management: create a registry of all assets (especially information assets).
  • Essential principle: You can‚Äôt protect what you don‚Äôt know you have.
  • Keeping it current is challenging, especially for all hosts, endpoints, and their data.

2. Baselines

  • Complete record of all system components (hardware, software, data, controls, docs, user instructions).
  • Used as a reference for comparison, updates, and development.
  • Supports minimum protection levels based on asset classification (e.g., high, medium, low).

3. Updates

  • Repairs, maintenance, and upgrades across infrastructure, OS, applications, and networks.
  • Require:
    • Acceptance testing (new functions work).
    • Regression testing (no new issues introduced).
  • Continuous security testing ensures updates keep systems secure.

4. Patches

  • Software/hardware modifications to fix vulnerabilities or improve functionality.
  • Challenges:
    • Multiple vendors, irregular schedules.
    • Risk of flawed patches‚Äîtesting is crucial.
    • Some patches critical ‚Üí deploy quickly; others less urgent but may be prerequisites.
  • Testing ideally in environments mirroring production (rare due to cost).
  • Rollback plans needed if a patch causes problems.
  • Automated patching can save time but risks unexpected outages.

The Risks of Change

  • Have a robust change management process in place.
  • Test changes in a model or testing environment before applying them to production.
  • Always prepare a rollback plan ‚Äì restore the system to the last known good state if something goes wrong.
  • Review and test patches before deployment; be able to restore previous configurations.
  • Maintaining a separate test environment can be challenging, so some organizations rely on vendor or third-party testing instead.
  • Rollback plans are especially critical when full testing isn‚Äôt possible before implementation.
