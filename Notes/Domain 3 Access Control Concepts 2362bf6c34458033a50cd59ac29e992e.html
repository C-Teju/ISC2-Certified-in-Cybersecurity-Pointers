Domain 3: Access Control Concepts

Security Controls

A control is a safeguard or countermeasure to preserve the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability of data—collectively known as the CIA Triad.

Access control limits which subjects (users or systems) can access which objects (data or resources) based on defined rules.

A common example is a firewall, which acts as a control to block unauthorized external access to a network and prevent sensitive internal data from leaving the system.

Which of the following is an example of security control?
Firewall

Controls Overview

Access control is about granting the appropriate level of access to authorized personnel and processes while denying access to unauthorized functions or individuals

.

Subjects

A subject is any active entity that initiates a request to access an asset. This can be a user, process, program, client, server, or device like a workstation or smartphone.

Key points about a subject:

  • It actively requests access to a resource or service.
  • It includes users, software, and hardware with the ability to initiate actions.
  • It interacts with objects (resources) by requesting services.
  • It must have appropriate permissions or clearance to access the requested resource.

Objects

By definition, an object is anything a subject tries to access—such as a device, user, program, server, or process.

Unlike subjects, objects are passive; they do not initiate actions but respond to service requests. If a request is incorrect, the object’s response may not meet the subject’s intent.

Objects do not contain their own access control logic. Instead, they rely on external systems, like identity and access management, to protect them from unauthorized access

An object has an owner, who determines who or what can access it. Access rules are often defined in a rule base or an access control list .

Key points about an object:

  • It can be a building, computer, file, database, printer, server, memory block, I/O port, person, software task, thread, or process.
  • It provides a service to a user.
  • It is passive and responds only when a request is made.
  • It may have a classification level to define sensitivity.

Rules

An access rule is an instruction used to allow or deny a subject access to an object by comparing the subject’s validated identity against an access control list (ACL).

For example, in a firewall, rules determine which network traffic is allowed. By default, firewalls block all traffic. A rule might permit traffic from the inside network to the outside network—allowing the subject (inside network) access to the object (outside network). Similarly, when a user tries to access a file, access rules check what level of access is appropriate based on defined attributes.

An access rule can:

  • Compare multiple attributes to determine access
  • Allow or deny access to an object
  • Define the level of access (read, write, execute, etc.)
  • Apply time-based access restrictions

These rules help enforce access control policies across systems and resources.

Defense in Depth

Access control includes permissions to buildings, server rooms, networks, applications, and utilities. These are part of a layered defense strategy, known as defense in depth.

Defense in depth is a security approach that combines people, technology, and processes to create multiple barriers across the organization. It uses layered countermeasures to reduce risk, though it cannot fully prevent attacks.

Examples include:

  • Multi-factor authentication (e.g., password + phone code) using two layers: something you know and something you have.
  • Multiple firewalls to separate untrusted networks (like the internet) from trusted internal systems holding sensitive data.


When a company handles information with varying sensitivity levels, it may use multiple firewalls to enforce stricter access control. Network traffic is validated by rules at each layer, with the most sensitive data protected behind multiple firewall layers for added security.


A non-technical example of defense in depth is accessing data in a data center. First, a physical lock restricts entry. Second, technical controls block network access. Third, administrative policies define who is authorized to access the data.

Layered Defense

Examples of Least Privilege

Least Privilege ensures users have access only to the information and resources necessary for their roles. This principle, enforced through privileged access management, helps protect confidentiality by limiting unnecessary access.

For example, only billing staff can view consumer financial data, and even fewer can modify it—supporting confidentiality, integrity, and availability through proper authentication and permissions.

Access may also be limited by time, such as during business hours, or by data fields, like in healthcare where some staff can view patient details but not medical records. Doctors may only access their own patients' data, as required by privacy laws like HIPAA in USA and by specific privacy lays in other countries.

Systems often monitor access, triggering alerts if unauthorized attempts are detected, so security teams can respond accordingly.


The more critical the information a person can access, the stronger the security measures should be—such as enforcing multi-factor authentication to protect that access.

Mandatory Access Control (MAC) in the Workplace

Mandatory Access Control (MAC) restricts access based on predefined policies set by the asset owner, not individual judgment.

For example, government agencies require specific security clearances for access, as defined by government policy and not by the individual giving access based on their own judgement. This is often combined with separation of duties, limiting users to only the information needed for their role—often enforced through role-based access control.

In Mandatory Access Control (MAC), what determines the level of access to certain areas in certain government agencies?
Government policy and security clearance

Logical Access Controls

Physical access controls restrict entry to areas or assets using tangible methods, while logical access controls use electronic methods to restrict access to systems or data.

Examples of logical controls include:

  • Passwords
  • Biometrics (e.g., on phones or laptops)
  • Badge or token readers connected to systems

These controls ensure only authorized users can access assets, even if they have physical access.

Which of the following is an example of a logical access control method?
Biometrics on a smartphone

Defense in Depth in Practice

A data center uses multiple layers of defense: administrative controls (policies and procedures), logical controls (access restrictions), and physical controls (secure facilities).

Despite cloud and virtualization advancements, data is still stored and processed on physical hardware in physical locations, underscoring the importance of physical security.

Limiting access to data on the network would be considered which of the following controls?
Logical or technical controls

Controls and Risks

  1. A seatbelt is a physical control, while a law requiring its use is an administrative control. Together, they reduce driving risk to an acceptable level for both the individual and society.
  1. A tall bookshelf poses a risk of injury if it falls. A building code requiring it to be secured is an administrative control, while strapping it to the wall is a physical control. Together, they reduce the risk of harm.
What would be considered an administrative control in the context of seat belt usage?
Passing a law requiring seat belt use

Controls Assessments

Risk reduction depends on how effective a control is. It must be relevant to the current situation and adaptable to changing conditions.

Consider a scenario where part of an office building is being re-purposed for use as a secure storage facility. Due to the previous use of the area, there are 5 doors which must be secured before confidential files can be stored there.

When converting part of an office into a secure storage area, all five existing doors must be secured before storing confidential files.

Options include biometric scanners, but a site assessment will determine if that level of control is needed for all doors. Some doors could be permanently sealed or replaced with walls, depending on the budget and security needs.

Ultimately, the cost of controls should match the value of the protected data. If high-level controls like biometric locks aren’t necessary, simple deadbolts may be sufficient.

What alternative control could be used if biometric locks on multiple doors are not necessary and access does not need to be audited?
Replacing doors with deadbolt locks

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

Role-Based Access Control assigns access permissions to roles rather than individual users. When someone takes on a job, they're assigned a role with the necessary access. If they leave the role, access is automatically removed. This approach is efficient in environments with high staff turnover and similar access needs across roles.

In what type of environment does role-based access control work well?
High-staff turnover and similar access requirements

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) in the Workplace

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) assigns access based on a user's role in the organization. For example, HR can access personnel files, Finance manages bank accounts, and managers can view their teams’ information. New employees receive only the minimum access needed for their jobs, while system admins may have full access.

Monitoring role assignments is essential. If temporary access is given (e.g., a junior staff member acting as manager) and not revoked later, the next person in that role may inherit inappropriate permissions—a risk known as privilege creep or or permissions creep.

To avoid this:

  • Regularly audit and adjust role permissions.
  • Avoid copying existing user profiles when assigning roles.
  • Use standardized roles to ensure each new user starts with the correct level of access.

This helps maintain security and ensures employees only have access to what they need.

What term is used to describe the situation where someone inherits expanded permissions that are not appropriate for their role in Role-based Access Control (RBAC)?
privilege creep

Privileged Access Management

Privileged Access Management (PAM) controls and secures high-level access to critical systems. Instead of granting full administrative rights at all times, PAM uses just-in-time access—activating privileges only when needed for specific tasks.

Without PAM, admin rights are constantly active, increasing the risk of misuse or exploitation. For example, at ABC Inc., IT staff added their IDs to the Domain Admins group for convenience. When one employee opened a malicious email, the ransomware used their admin privileges to encrypt all systems.

With PAM in place, admin access would be temporary and task-specific, preventing the ransomware from spreading. Routine tasks like checking email would be performed with standard user rights, significantly reducing risk.

What is the key feature of just-in-time privileged access management?
Role-based subsets of privileges

Privileged Accounts

Privileged Accounts are user accounts with elevated permissions beyond those of standard users. These are typically granted to trusted personnel who need enhanced access to perform specialized tasks.

Common users of privileged accounts include:

  • System Administrators – manage OS, applications, and performance.
  • Help Desk/IT Support – access endpoints and systems for troubleshooting.
  • Security Analysts – need broad access for threat detection and response.
  • Project/Client-Specific Roles – granted temporary or project-based control.

Such access must be carefully assigned, monitored, and revoked when no longer needed, as misuse of privileged accounts can cause serious organizational harm.

To moderate risks from misuse or abuse of privileged accounts, organizations implement stricter controls than for regular user accounts. Key measures include:

  1. Detailed Logging:
    • Every action by privileged users is extensively logged.
    • Serves as both a deterrent and a detective control (for audits and investigations).
  1. Stricter Access Control:
    • Privileged users undergo more rigorous authentication, such as advanced MFA.
    • Just-in-time access grants privileges only when needed and revokes them afterward.
  1. Stronger Trust Verification:
    • Privileged users may be required to pass thorough background checks, NDAs, and financial vetting.
    • Checks may be updated periodically or triggered by events.
  1. Enhanced Auditing:
    • Activity is more frequently and deeply audited to catch inappropriate access or risky behavior early.

These controls collectively help prevent, detect, and respond to threats stemming from misuse of elevated privileges.

Privileged Access Management (PAM) – Help Desk Example :

Help Desk staff often need to reset passwords or unlock accounts. Instead of giving full "domain admin" access, they can be granted only those specific permissions. All actions should be logged and regularly audited—for example, by matching daily password reset logs with support tickets for password reset—to ensure secure, appropriate use without over-privileging.

Monitoring

The use of physical access controls, monitoring personnel and equipment entering and leaving, and auditing and logging all physical events are primary elements in maintaining overall organizational security.

Examples:

  • Cameras: Provide live surveillance and recordings; deter threats and support forensics.
  • Motion Sensors: Use infrared, microwave, or vibration tech to detect movement or breaches.
  • Logs: Physical/electronic records of entries; essential for audits, legal compliance, and anomaly detection. Logs must be reviewed, retained per policy, and protected from tampering.
  • Alarm Systems: Detect unauthorized access (e.g., forced entry), fire, or emergencies via sensors and alert appropriate personnel.
  • Panic Buttons: Manual emergency alerts for immediate police/security response.
  • Security Guards: Act as a deterrent, verify identity, prevent tailgating, and respond to physical threats.

These combined methods ensure comprehensive monitoring and protection of physical assets and premises.
